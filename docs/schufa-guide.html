What is SCHUFA?

SCHUFA stands for Schutzgemeinschaft für allgemeine Kreditsicherung — the “protection association for general credit security.” Founded in 1927, it is Germany’s dominant credit reference agency, holding data on roughly 68 million people and 6 million companies.

Every time you open a German bank account, sign a mobile phone contract, take out a loan, or activate a credit card, the provider reports this to SCHUFA. Over time, SCHUFA builds a profile of your creditworthiness based on how you manage financial obligations. Landlords, banks, and telecom providers use this profile to decide whether to do business with you — and on what terms.

For expats arriving in Germany, SCHUFA is often the first and most frustrating bureaucratic wall. No SCHUFA history means you are invisible to the system, and most providers interpret that invisibility as risk.

How the score is calculated

SCHUFA does not publish its exact algorithm. However, from regulatory disclosures and independent research, the primary factors and approximate weightings are well understood:

FactorWeightWhat SCHUFA looks for
Payment history~35%On-time payments on all contracts
Account age & duration~25%How long you have had German financial accounts
Credit mix~15%Variety: bank account, mobile, credit card
Number of accounts~12%1–3 active accounts is optimal
Credit inquiries~8%Hard inquiries lower the score temporarily
Address stability~5%Frequent address changes are a mild negative

The output is a single percentage between 0% and 100%. SCHUFA expresses this as the probability of not defaulting — so higher is always better.

How to check your SCHUFA for free

Under GDPR Article 15, every EU resident is entitled to a free copy of all data a company holds about them. SCHUFA calls this the Datenkopie. To request it: visit meineschufa.de → “Datenkopie (DSGVO)” → create an account → verify your identity via PostIdent or VideoIdent → wait 5–7 business days.

💡
Pro tip: Request your free Datenkopie in January each year. Check it for errors — incorrect negative entries are more common than people realise, and each one can be disputed and removed within 30 days.

The expat cold-start problem

When you arrive in Germany, SCHUFA has no record of you. This is called a “thin file” — and it is arguably harder to deal with than a bad score. A bad score is interpretable. A missing score is unknown risk, and risk-averse German institutions default to rejection.

The practical consequences hit fast. No SCHUFA entry means many landlords will reject your application outright. Traditional banks may not open a full current account. Phone providers push you toward prepaid. The good news: building SCHUFA is a learnable, manageable process. It takes time — not luck.

12-month build-up roadmap

Month 1–2
Foundation

Open a German bank account immediately. Choose a bank that reports to SCHUFA and does not require a SCHUFA check to open: N26, DKB, or ING are the best starting points. This creates your first positive entry and starts the account-age clock. Complete your Anmeldung simultaneously — it is legally required within 14 days and unlocks most financial products.

Month 2–3
First contract

Get a German postpaid mobile contract. Even a basic SIM-only plan (€10–15/month) creates a second SCHUFA entry and demonstrates that a provider trusted you. Make sure it is postpaid — prepaid is not reported. Providers like Congstar or O2 are more lenient with thin files than Telekom directly.

Month 4–6
Credit signals

Apply for a revolving credit card from your existing bank. Use it for regular purchases and pay it off in full every month. This is the single highest-impact action for your SCHUFA score. The ING Visa Card or DKB Visa are accessible with thin files and both report positively.

Month 6–12
Consistency

Pay everything on time, every time. Set up SEPA direct debits (Lastschrift) wherever possible. Do not apply for multiple new accounts simultaneously — each hard inquiry slightly lowers your score, and multiple applications in a short period is a red flag to SCHUFA’s algorithm.

What damages your SCHUFA score

  • Missed or late payments — Any payment 60+ days overdue that is reported after a written reminder creates a negative entry that stays for 3 years after settlement.
  • Multiple credit inquiries in a short period — Applying to 3 banks in one month signals financial distress. Space applications at least 6 months apart.
  • Closing old accounts — Your oldest account is your most valuable. Never close your first German bank account.
  • Address instability — Moving frequently signals higher default risk. Always re-register immediately (Ummeldung is legally required within 14 days).
  • Insolvency proceedings — A formal Insolvenzverfahren stays on SCHUFA for 6 years from discharge. This effectively blocks most financial products.

Frequently asked questions

Can I get a SCHUFA entry before my Anmeldung? +
Technically yes — some banks (notably N26) will open an account with a foreign address. However, most financial products that create SCHUFA entries require a German address. Your Anmeldung should be your first administrative priority.
What is a good SCHUFA score for renting a flat? +
Most landlords want 95%+. In Munich or Berlin you are competing against applicants at 97–99%. If your score is below 90%, offer 2–3 months additional deposit or a co-signer (Bürge).
Does checking my own SCHUFA damage the score? +
No. Self-checks (Datenkopie) are soft inquiries and have zero impact. Only provider-initiated hard inquiries for credit applications affect the score, and only temporarily (12 months).
Can I transfer credit history from my home country? +
Not directly. SCHUFA holds only German data. Some services like Nova Credit offer translated credit passports, but adoption by German providers is limited. You must build SCHUFA from scratch using the roadmap above.
How long does negative SCHUFA data stay on record? +
Paid debts: 3 years. Insolvency: 6 years from discharge. Hard credit inquiries: 1 year. Account closures: 3 years after closure.
