What is actually happening

Many expats in Germany face the challenge of their foreign degrees not being recognized or valued in the local job market. This, combined with student debt, can be overwhelming. However, Germany offers various resources for expats looking to make a career change, such as the Federal Employment Agency (Bundesagentur für Arbeit) and local job centers that can provide guidance on recognizing foreign qualifications and finding employment. Understanding your current financial situation, including your SCHUFA score, is crucial. A good SCHUFA score can open doors to better loan options and credit deals, which can be particularly beneficial when managing student debt. Starting to explore new career paths that are in demand in Germany can also be a proactive step. Fields like tech, healthcare, and sustainability are often looking for skilled professionals.

Why this happens to expats in Germany

The process of recognizing foreign degrees in Germany can be complex and time-consuming. Expats often find themselves in a situation where their qualifications are not directly transferable to the German job market, leading to a career change. Moreover, the pressure of student debt, which can affect one's SCHUFA score, adds another layer of complexity. Having a German bank account, specifically a blocked account (Sperrkonto) for managing finances related to your student debt and everyday expenses, can help in keeping track of your financial health. It's essential for expats to understand the German job market and how their skills can be adapted or further developed to fit into this market. Many universities and colleges in Germany offer additional courses or certifications that can enhance one's employability.

Step-by-step: how to fix it

The first step towards a career change in Germany is to assess your current situation, including your financial health and career aspirations. This involves checking your SCHUFA score and understanding how it affects your ability to get loans or credit. Next, identify the skills you have that are transferable to the German job market. Consider fields that are in high demand and where your skills can be valuable. The Federal Employment Agency can provide insights into the current job market trends. Applying for recognition of your foreign degree (Anerkennung) is a crucial step. This process can vary depending on the profession and the state (Bundesland) you're in. Some professions require a full recognition, while others may allow you to work with a partial recognition or under certain conditions.

What to do if you're stuck

If you're feeling stuck, seeking advice from career counselors or coaches who specialize in working with expats in Germany can be incredibly helpful. They can provide personalized guidance on career transition, resume building, and interview preparation tailored to the German job market. Networking is also key. Attend job fairs, industry events, and join professional networks or clubs related to your desired field. This can help you meet potential employers or mentors who can offer valuable advice or opportunities. Lastly, don't underestimate the power of learning German. While many Germans speak English, proficiency in German can significantly improve your job prospects and help you integrate more smoothly into German society and the workforce.

Quick tip: Start by checking your SCHUFA score and understanding its implications on your financial health and career change plans. You can request a free SCHUFA report once a year, which can give you insights into your creditworthiness and help you manage your finances more effectively, including your student debt and German bank account.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to get a foreign degree recognized in Germany?
The recognition process can vary, but generally, it takes a few months to a year. It's essential to start this process as early as possible to plan your career change effectively. Some professions may have a faster track, especially those in high demand.

Can I work in Germany while my degree is being recognized?
Yes, in some cases, you can work in Germany while your degree is being recognized, especially if you're in a field that doesn't require full recognition to start working, such as IT or arts. However, this depends on the specific regulations of your profession and the state you're in.

How does my SCHUFA score affect my ability to get a loan for further education in Germany?
A good SCHUFA score can significantly improve your chances of getting a loan for further education. Banks and financial institutions in Germany use the SCHUFA score to assess the creditworthiness of loan applicants. Maintaining a good score by paying bills on time and managing debt responsibly is crucial.
