What is a Blocked Account (Sperrkonto)?

A Sperrkonto — literally "blocked account" — is a special bank account required by German immigration authorities as proof of financial resources. It is mandatory for most non-EU international students applying for a German student visa or residence permit. You deposit a set amount of money before arriving in Germany, and that money is "blocked" — meaning it cannot be withdrawn freely. Once you arrive, you receive a fixed monthly payout (€992 in 2026) for living expenses.

It is not a regular bank account. You cannot use it for daily spending, card payments, or transfers. Its sole purpose is to prove financial solvency to the German embassy during the visa process, and to then release a controlled monthly allowance once you are in Germany.

🇩🇪 Blocked Account vs Regular Bank Account

These are two completely separate things. Your Sperrkonto is for visa compliance and monthly releases. Your regular Girokonto (e.g. N26, DKB) is for daily banking — rent, groceries, transfers. You need both. Most blocked account providers offer a Girokonto as part of their package — but you are not required to use theirs.

The €11,904 Requirement in 2026

The German government sets the minimum deposit annually based on the BAföG standard rate. For 2026, the requirement is €11,904 — calculated as €992 per month × 12 months. This amount is reviewed annually and may change. The €992/month withdrawal mirrors the maximum BAföG student support rate.

In addition to the €11,904 deposit, most providers require an additional buffer amount (€80–€100) which is held briefly and returned with your first monthly payout. Plan your total transfer accordingly.

⚠ Common Mistake
Depositing exactly €11,904 is not enough

You must deposit €11,904 plus the provider's setup fee (billed separately) plus the buffer (if required). Fintiba requires a €100 buffer. Expatrio requires no buffer. Factor in the international transfer fee on top of everything. Many students arrive short by €100–€200 and face delays.

5 Mistakes That Cause Visa Rejection

These are the real-world patterns that cause embassy rejections. None of them are about the money. All of them are preventable.

01

Name mismatch between account and passport

Your name on the blocking confirmation must match your passport exactly — every middle name, every hyphen, every accent character. "Mohamed A. Rahman" ≠ "Mohamed Abdel Rahman".

Fix: Before applying, write out your full name exactly as it appears in your passport. Copy-paste it into the application form. Do not abbreviate.
02

Applying too late — no certificate by visa appointment date

The full process takes 2–4 weeks. Students who apply 1–2 weeks before their visa appointment frequently cannot provide the blocking confirmation in time.

Fix: Apply at least 6–8 weeks before your visa appointment. 8 weeks is the safe target. International wire transfers alone can take 5–10 business days.
03

Depositing the wrong amount

Students transfer exactly €11,904 and forget the buffer, or the transfer loses €15–€50 in intermediary bank fees, arriving as €11,854. The provider rejects the deposit as insufficient.

Fix: Add €150 to whatever amount you transfer to cover hidden intermediary fees. Excess above the required amount is simply returned. Using Wise eliminates most intermediary fee surprises.
04

Wrong document version submitted to the embassy

Providers issue two documents: a preliminary confirmation (Vorläufige Bestätigung) and a final blocking confirmation (Sperrbestätigung). Some students submit the preliminary one, which is not accepted by embassies.

Fix: Wait for the official Sperrbestätigung — the document explicitly stating "Sperrbestätigung" with the full €11,904 amount shown as blocked. Do not submit any preliminary or provisional confirmation.
05

Applying to a suspended or unrecognized provider

Coracle is suspended. Deutsche Bank stopped in 2022. Some students still apply to these providers based on outdated guide articles, receive no confirmation, and miss their visa appointment entirely.

Fix: In 2026, only apply to Fintiba or Expatrio. Verify on the provider's own website that they are actively accepting new applications before you start.

Step-by-Step: How to Open Your Blocked Account

01

Go to Expatrio.com or Fintiba.com — not a comparison site

Apply directly through the official provider website. This ensures you get the current pricing, application form, and IBAN for your transfer. Third-party sites may have outdated information.

02

Upload your documents

You need: (1) passport main page — full name, photo, expiry date visible, (2) university admission letter or Zulassungsbescheid. That is typically all that is required at this stage.

03

Receive your provider IBAN and transfer the funds

You will receive a dedicated IBAN specifically for your blocked account. Transfer €11,904 (plus buffer if Fintiba) to this IBAN. Use Wise for the transfer — see section below for the cost comparison.

04

Receive your Sperrbestätigung

This is the official blocking confirmation certificate. It states your name, the blocked amount (€11,904), and the provider's bank details. This document goes into your visa application.

05

Submit with visa application — then activate on arrival

After visa approval and arrival in Germany, activate your account by providing your registered German address and university enrollment certificate. Monthly €992 payouts begin.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much money do I need in my blocked account in 2026?▼
The mandatory deposit for 2026 is €11,904 — €992 × 12 months, based on the BAföG standard rate. On top of this, add your provider's buffer (Fintiba: €100, Expatrio: none) and a small safety margin for transfer fees. Total transfer recommendation: €12,050–€12,100.
Is Coracle still available in 2026?▼
No. Coracle suspended all new blocked account applications in August 2025 due to a banking system migration. There is no confirmed date for when (or if) they will resume. Do not apply to Coracle. The only currently active providers are Fintiba and Expatrio.
Which is better — Fintiba or Expatrio?▼
For most students, Expatrio is the better value: €49 setup fee, fast processing, free checking account option, and no buffer required. Choose Fintiba if: you come from a high-risk country where embassy scrutiny is higher, you are under 18, you specifically need phone support, or the German banking status (German deposit protection) is important to you.
How long does it take to get the blocking confirmation?▼
After your funds arrive at the provider: Fintiba issues the confirmation instantly. Expatrio takes 1–3 business days. The international money transfer itself takes 1–10 business days depending on your country and method. Total process: 2–4 weeks. Apply at minimum 6 weeks before your visa appointment — 8 weeks is safer.
What happens to my money if my visa is rejected?▼
Both providers offer a refund in case of visa refusal. Your €11,904 deposit is fully returned. The refund processing fee is €89 (Fintiba) or €109 (Expatrio). Refund timeline is typically 4 weeks. Your setup fee is not refunded.
Can I use Wise to send money to my blocked account?▼
Yes, and it is strongly recommended. Wise uses the mid-market exchange rate with a transparent fee of approximately 0.4–1%. On a €11,904 transfer, you save €120–€180 compared to a traditional bank wire. Both Fintiba and Expatrio explicitly support Wise transfers.
Do I need the blocked account provider's Girokonto too?▼
No — it is optional. Expatrio offers a free Girokonto as part of its package, which can be convenient. But you are not required to use it. Many students prefer to open a separate regular bank account (N26, DKB) for daily banking while keeping the blocked account purely for visa compliance and monthly releases.
